Security · ML

Why I threat-model my machine-learning projects

When I started building classifiers, I optimised the same thing everyone does: a single accuracy number on a test set. It took shipping FakeAlert for me to internalise that, for any model that touches people, accuracy is close to a vanity metric. A content-moderation model can score 95% and still be a liability.

So now I run an ML project through the same lens I'd use for any system that has adversaries — because it does. Concretely, I ask three questions:

1. Who does it fail on? Aggregate error hides distributional harm. A toxicity model that's 95% accurate overall can be 80% accurate on a minority dialect — meaning it silences the very people it was meant to protect. I measure error per group and treat the gap as a defect, not a footnote.

2. How easily is it fooled? Anyone trying to evade a filter will reach for character swaps, spacing, homoglyphs, and paraphrase. If I haven't generated those perturbations and measured the accuracy drop, I don't actually know how the model performs in the wild — I know how it performs against opponents who aren't trying.

3. What does it leak or retain? Training data can be memorised; inference pipelines can quietly hoard personal data. Data minimisation and a clear retention story are part of the model's design, not the ops team's problem later.

The shift

Threat-modelling a model — evasion, poisoning, membership inference, privacy leakage — consistently produces a better product than chasing a leaderboard. It's the most direct place my security background makes my engineering better.

Security · UX

Designing PhishBlock: security for people who aren't technical

I built PhishBlock after watching people I care about — none of them engineers — get caught by phishing that any security person would spot instantly. The lesson that stuck: the detection is the easy half. The hard half is getting a non-expert to understand and act on a warning before they click.

A few principles came out of it:

  • Explain the "why", not just the verdict. "This link is risky because it impersonates a bank domain" beats a red cross. People follow advice they understand.
  • Make the safe path the easy path. If avoiding the risk is more work than taking it, users take the risk. The secure action has to be one tap.
  • Assume fear and shame. People who've been scammed often don't report it. The tone has to be supportive, never "you should have known."

This is the thread running through all my security work: the people most exposed to online harm are usually the least equipped to defend themselves. A tool that only a technical user can operate isn't protecting the people who need it most.

Privacy · Humanitarian tech

Securing humanitarian data: lessons from GetRespondr

GetRespondr coordinates crisis response — incident reports, volunteers, resources. Building it forced a realisation that reframed how I think about data: in a humanitarian context, the data itself is the threat surface. A list of who reported what, from where, during a conflict or disaster is not a database — it's a target.

That changes the defaults:

  • Minimise first. The most secure field is the one you never collect. Every data point has to justify the risk it creates for the person it describes.
  • Least privilege, by role. A volunteer coordinating tasks does not need to see reporter identities. Access is scoped to the job, not the org.
  • Design for the worst case. Assume the system could be seized or breached, and ask: what's the damage to the most vulnerable person in the dataset? Then engineer to shrink that.

This is exactly where my two worlds meet. The years of humanitarian work taught me who gets hurt and how; the security training taught me how to build so they don't. That intersection — protection as an engineering discipline — is the work I want to keep doing.

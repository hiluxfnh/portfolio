FakeAlert — detecting disinformation without doing more harm

An NLP system that flags fake news and hate speech for social integrity — designed so that bias, privacy, and adversarial abuse are treated as security problems, not afterthoughts.

Context

Disinformation and online hate aren't abstract problems for the communities I work with — they translate into real-world harm: manipulated elections, eroded trust in health information, and targeted abuse of women and minorities. I'd spent years on the policy side of this through UN forums. FakeAlert was my attempt to attack the same problem from the engineering side, and it became the basis of my 2025 paper, "NLP in the Digital Age: Combating Fake News, Hate Speech, and Ethical Risks for Social Integrity."

The problem

Most content-classification systems optimise a single number — accuracy — and quietly inherit three dangerous failure modes:

  • They encode bias. Models trained on naive datasets routinely flag African-American English or minority dialects as "toxic" at higher rates, silencing exactly the people they should protect.
  • They are trivially gamed. Adversaries evade filters with character swaps, spacing, and paraphrase — so a model that scores well offline collapses in the wild.
  • They over-collect. Moderation pipelines hoover up personal data, creating a privacy liability and a chilling effect.

My goal wasn't "the highest F1 on a benchmark." It was a classifier that is useful, fair, robust, and privacy-respecting — and honest about its limits.

Goals & constraints

  • Detect both misinformation and hate speech in a single pipeline.
  • Measure and reduce demographic bias, not just overall error.
  • Survive simple adversarial perturbations.
  • Run on modest hardware (a student budget, not a GPU farm).
  • Be explainable — a human moderator must see why something was flagged.

Approach

Data

I combined public misinformation and hate-speech corpora, then deliberately stress-tested them for representation gaps before training — because a model is only as fair as the distribution it learns from.

Modelling

I built up in layers rather than jumping to the heaviest model:

  1. A transparent TF-IDF + linear baseline, to establish a floor and a sanity check on features.
  2. A fine-tuned transformer encoder (BERT-family) for context-sensitive classification.
  3. An explainability layer surfacing the tokens driving each decision, so flags are auditable.

Security & ethics — treated as first-class

This is where FakeAlert differs from a typical ML project, and where my security background shows up:

Bias as a measured metric

I evaluated error rates per group, not just overall, and tuned thresholds to shrink the gap — so the system doesn't disproportionately silence minority voices.

Adversarial robustness

I generated perturbed inputs (character swaps, spacing, homoglyphs) and folded a normalisation step in front of the classifier, treating evasion as an attacker would.

Privacy by design

The pipeline classifies content without persisting personal identifiers, and is built to keep a human in the loop — automation assists moderation, it never replaces accountability.

Evaluation

I evaluated on a held-out split using precision, recall and F1 per class, plus a fairness gap across demographic slices and an adversarial-robustness delta (clean vs. perturbed accuracy). The full results and methodology are in the published paper.

What I learned

  • Accuracy is a vanity metric in moderation. The interesting questions are "who does it fail on?" and "how easily is it fooled?"
  • Security thinking transfers. Threat-modelling an ML system (evasion, poisoning, privacy leakage) produced a stronger product than chasing a leaderboard.
  • Keep humans in the loop. The right role for the model is to triage and explain, not to judge.

What's next

  • Extend multilingual coverage (English + French first, given my context).
  • Harden against poisoning, and publish a model card documenting limits and intended use.
  • Deepen this line of work through my MSc in Information Security at ITMO University.
